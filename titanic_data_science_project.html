4.3 Explain your methodology: how did you arrive at these conclusions?¶

To get to these answers I first cleaned and prepared the data by handling missing values in Age and Embarked, and removing the sparsely populated Cabin column. I then engineered new features like Title (from the Name) and FamilySize (from SibSp, Parch and last name counts) to capture social and family dynamics. I binned the continuous variables like Age into meaningful categories (AgeGroup) to see the trends. I used groupby() and value_counts(normalize=True) and bar and line plots to compare the survival rates across the key attributes: gender, class, age group and family size. This combination of feature engineering, groupby and visualization allowed me to draw clear data driven insights about the survival patterns on the Titanic.

5. Conclusion and Reflection¶

5.1 Conclusion¶

Answer to the Main task - Central Question:

The groups of passengers most likely to survive were women, children and first class. Women had a much higher survival rate than men because of the evacuation policies that prioritized women and children. Children, infants and younger age groups had the best chance of survival. First class passengers were more likely to survive than second or third class, probably because of their proximity to the lifeboats and more access to the crew. Passengers with small to medium sized families (2-4 people) also had better survival outcomes than those traveling alone or with large families, possibly because of mutual help and ease of coordination during the evacuation.

Key Takeaways:

  • Gender was the biggest factor. Women survived way more than men. Just like the “women and children first” evacuation policy.

  • Class mattered. 1st class passengers survived way more than 2nd or 3rd class. Socioeconomic status.

  • Age was a factor, especially for infants and children who were prioritized.

  • Family size was non-linear:

  • Solo travelers had lower survival rates (likely because they didn’t have a support group).

  • Small to moderate families (2-4 people) had the highest survival rates.

  • Larger families had lower survival odds. Maybe because of coordination challenges during the disaster.

5.2 Reflection¶

Missing data was handled with care:

  • Age was imputed using group medians based on Title and Pclass.
  • Embarked was filled using the mode.
